1. Cable Modeling

1.1 Catenary

A catenary is a curve that can be used to model the position and tension of an overhead cable. It is defined by the following:

  • Horizontal tension (H)
  • Unit weight (w)
  • Horizontal spacing, from left end point to right end point (A)
  • Vertical spacing, from left end point to right end point (B)



The catenary tension changes along the curve. The lowpoint has the lowest tension, and the tension increases moving toward either end. The horizontal tension (H) is the tension at the lowpoint, while the support tensions are at the catenary ends. The unit weight (w) is the weight per linear foot of the cable. Together, the horizontal tension and the unit weight define the shape of the curve (H/w). For a fixed spacing, a lower H/w will sag deeper than a catenary with a higher H/w, as shown below.



1.2 Temperature / Tension

Most overhead cables respond to temperature change. As the temperature varies, the cable material will either contract or elongate. To account for this, installation tensions are typically provided for a wide range of temperatures.

The temperature-tension points will be curve fitted to fill in the gaps for all temperatures within range. The curve needs to have a specific shape, as shown below. When temperature increases, the tension should drop quickly at first and then slowly taper off.



1.3 Creep Correction

The creep correction is a cable tension correction that accounts for long-term permanent elongation of the cable. It adjusts the tension so that the cable is installed tighter than the cable-tension points discussed above. It is reported as a temperature, indicating that the cable should be installed at a set number of degrees colder than the actual cable temperature.



1.4 Sag Correction

The sag correction is a cable tension correction that accounts for the cable hanging differently in travelers compared to when it is clipped in. This is caused by uneven spans and is most often seen in multi-span sag sections where the cable runs downhill into the largest or lowest span.

The sag correction is added to the nominal sag of the catenary. Positive sag corrections will increase the sag (and decrease the tension), while negative sag corrections will have the opposite effect.



1.5 Scale Factor

The scaling factor is a cable tension correction that allows pre-stressing tensions to be calculated. This is factor usually is between 1.0 and 1.5, depending on how long the cable will be pre-stressed.
